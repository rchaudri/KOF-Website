Fremont, CA — Where the Name Came From

The Kid Who Named Himself King

He called himself the King of Fremont. Nobody handed him that — he walked into a chess park on the Berkeley campus at 18 and introduced himself to a crowd that had every reason to ignore him. That crowd drove in from Oakland, played for money, ran old-school hip-hop on speakers, and didn’t want newcomers. He showed up anyway and gave them the name. They adopted it.

That’s the origin of everything on this page. Not a brand strategy, not a persona workshop — a name offered with complete seriousness in a room where seriousness got tested against real competition. He was 18 and from Fremont and he walked in like he already knew how it ended.

From that point: six years of work built entirely on his own logic, his own timeline, his own system. Operating outside every conventional lane. Moving at a speed the industry wasn’t watching for, from a city it had never once thought about.

Content Architecture — The Cipher

The Chessboard Feed

Most people look at his Instagram grid and see a nice-looking feed. Look closer and you see a chessboard. Look closer still and you realize the chess is real — a complete, legally valid game being played one post at a time, from opening through complex middlegame to a designed endgame. And underneath that, every other post is a chapter of his actual life. Three simultaneous layers, running without error, for years.

Here’s what that requires: Instagram fills its grid newest-first in three columns. Every time you post, every previous image shifts one slot to the right. That means the column your new post lands in is determined by how many posts you’ve already made — mod 3, running in reverse of the reading direction. Get it wrong once and the entire visual checkerboard breaks retroactively, above every single post that came before it. He tracked this calculation across 150+ posts. The grid is flawless.

The chess game ends in a victorious position. He designed it that way from post one — encoding his own eventual win into the architecture of the feed before he had a single follower. That’s not content strategy. That’s a worldview written in code.

The game is still in his feed right now. You can look at it.

Christopher Thach saw it, understood the structure, and made a documentary. That’s what happens when someone finally looks closely enough.

The Game is Real

Every chess post is a legal move forward from the last one. Real openings, real middlegame, real endgame. If you know chess, you can follow every move through the feed. It ends in a win. He planned it that way from post one.

Engineering Project — Age 12 to 18

Myoelectric Bionic Hand Prototype

At 12 years old, Rohan started building a working myoelectric bionic hand. Surface EMG sensors on the forearm reading electrical signals from muscle activation. Custom classification software mapping those signals to specific finger movements. A 3D-printed mechanical hand that actually moved based on what the wearer intended to do. He wrote the software himself.

He worked on it for six years out of Horner Junior High in Fremont. Won at the Alameda County Science & Engineering Fair. Presented at the 2016 California State Science Fair. The pattern he established there — pick the problem that seems unsolvable, stay with it until it isn’t — runs through everything he’s built since.

Signal Processing

Your forearm muscles fire in different electrical patterns depending on which finger you’re moving. Rohan figured out how to read those patterns. At 12.

Mechanical Design

3D-printed frame, stepper motors, strings running like tendons. When the software said “index finger,” the index finger moved. Real articulation. Not a demo.

Control Software

He wrote the classification code himself. Different electrical signal patterns mapped to different finger movements, running in real time. At a middle school in Fremont.

The Thesis — Debut Album

The Scientific Artist

At 12 he was building bionic hands. At 18 he was encoding chess games into Instagram grids. At 22 he was making performance videos that stopped The Weeknd mid-scroll. The same brain, the same city, the same refusal to operate inside a single category.

His debut album is called The Scientific Artist because that’s the most accurate description he could give himself. The thesis this whole page proves: data rigor and genuine creative instinct aren’t opposites. They’re the same force when the person is wired a certain way. The chessboard grid is a data structure. The release timing was an experiment. The engagement was tracked. The crying on camera was real.

His music — tracks like “Inheritance” — moves between registers that most artists can’t hold at once. Soulful and hard, analytical and raw. It’s on Genius, Spotify, Apple Music. He’s a Berkeley-trained data scientist who is genuinely an artist. That’s the record, not the pitch.

The Album — January 25, 2023

The Artifact

The cover is a childhood photo. Not a press shot — Rohan as a kid, sitting on a park bench, looking directly into the camera with the kind of quiet seriousness that most adults spend years trying to perform. He designed the whole thing himself, alone.

The typography is the argument. “The Scientific” runs in rigid blue block letters across the middle of the frame — controlled, analytical, cold. “ARTIST” hits below it in loose white graffiti script — expressive, freeform, street. Two fonts that shouldn’t work together. They work because the tension between them is the concept. This isn’t a decoration of the idea. It’s the idea.

The Parental Advisory label sits on the bottom right, next to the face of a child. That’s deliberate. The child grew up to make something complicated and adult and honest, and he wanted both on the same page.

January 25, 2023. It appeared. Three weeks later he pulled it — no announcement, no explanation, no forwarding address. The album is gone. What you’re looking at is the entire record: the cover he designed himself, the date it happened, the idea that was his before anyone else touched it. That window has closed. If you were there, you were there.
